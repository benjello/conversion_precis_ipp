
Since the beginning of the 1990's, the unemployment rate has remained high in France, rising heated debate on the labor cost and its role in the difficulties of creating jobs for firms. Successive governments in office since the beginning of the 1990's in France have introduced reductions in employers' social security contributions. The idea behind this reform was that decreasing the labor cost would foster job creation, or, at least, avoid job destruction. The reduction in employers' social security contributions has a cost in the short term for public accounts, as the Social security scheme collects less contributions. However, in the mid and long run, the effect expected is that increase in employment and growth triggered by the reduction in contributions would generate more revenues for the Social security scheme as well as for the State budget (\textit{via a rise in taxes collected and a decrease in social transfers}).

\subsection{The \og TEPA \fg~ law (2007 - 2012)}

Instituted by the Fillon government, the law promoting work, employment and purchasing power (said law TEPA) set up a social and tax regime as an exception for overtime hours (for full-time employees) and for additional hours (for part-time employees)\footnote{Additional hours should not be worked on a regular and invariable basis.}. \subsubsection*{Income tax exemption}

As long as the TEPA law was into force, earnings paid out to workers under overtime are not part of the income tax base. \subsection{Modification on January 1\up{st}, 2012}

Tax exemption of overtime hours has been criticized both for its cost for government finance (about 5 billion euros a year) and for its assumed lack of efficiency \footnote{The debate on this topic has been harsh: on the one hand, for some people, in particular \citet{Heyer2001}, the rise observed in reported overtime hours has been largely due to declaration and windfall effects.
